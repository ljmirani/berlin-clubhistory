A short History of Berlins Club Scene

By Jaya Mirani

"Have you ever been to Berghain?"
That must be one of the most frequently asked questions I get, when I tell people abroad, that I live in Berlin. And it is understandable: The mystery of what is going on behind the thick concrete walls of the old factory, the tales of drug fueled, dance filled nights and days have made Berghain world famous. But it is obviously not the only (and for some not even the best) of the myriad of clubs in Berlin. I have collected a few of them* on this map:

* This data is collected from the cities tourist page - however there are many different interpretations of what constitutes a club and many that are not featured, so the data for this project is not a complete list of all clubs, but rather showing a few important ones, which also show some prevalent trends in the scene.


The capital of Germany has cemented it`s image as the capital of Clubs in the last 30 Years. But the clubs have more meaning than just being places to dance and drink. They are political spaces as well, spaces, where societal standards of the outside world are turned on it´s head (sometimes), for many marginalised groups, they were and are still safe spaces (again, not always the case). This year Berlin’s techno culture even became part of UNESCO’s intangible heritage, which protects rituals, traditions, and festivities from all over the world. The history of the Berlin Clubscene is also deeply intertwined with the turbulent history of the city itself. For this project I am going to show a bit of this history.

60s - 90s: East and West Berlin

The Beginnings of the modern club scene date back to the 60s and 70s in Berlin. Back then the city was divided between two countries, seperated by a wall - East Berlin as part of the GDR and West Berlin as part of the FRG.

Back then, the first clubs started to pop up on both sides of the wall.

There was quite a bit of competition between the eastern and western club scene. East Berlin kept pushing back the closing time of its bars so that they stayed open longer than those in the west. This eventually led the abolishment of closing times in West Berlin.

East Berlin was more restrictive when it came to music choices. Dance events had to adhere to a 60/40 rule when selecting music (60 percent East, 40 percent West music)

One of the most famous clubs during that time was the "Chez Romy Haag", considered to be one of the of modern club culture. The transsexual performer Romy Haag opened the club in 1974 after she found it hard to find a venue for performing. The club was frequented by many stars, such as David Bowie, Tina Turner or Grace Jones.

The city was in disarray, in the almost anarchical Zustände around the time of the reunification of Germany the ownership of many places in East Berlin was unclear.

There were many abandoned factories and no one watching over them - an adventure land for the dance-hungry people. Run down cellars became celebrated clubs.

One of the most famous examples is the club "Tresor". An old bank vault in a nondescript building on the then empty (and today very busy) Potsdamer Platz, a place formerly divided by the wall. Between the rusty metal rods Techno arrived in Berlin from Detroit.

90s - today: Dying of the Clubs

The expansion of clubscene in the early 90s did not last. The city is changing and the number of clubs appears to be steadily declining, making Clubsterben (‘club demise’) a real political issue. This map shows some important clubs closing in the years after 1990.

In the years after the Fall of the Wall, many of the provisional clubs closed - some of them were only meant to last a short time.

In the years from 2000 until 2010 the club scene remained relativly steady.

In the 2010s something began which became known as "Clubsterben" ("club demise"), many clubs had to close due to noise complaints and gentrification.

The pandemic also hit many clubs hard.
